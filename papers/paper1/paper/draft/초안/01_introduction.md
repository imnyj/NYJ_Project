# Section I. Introduction

## I. Introduction

The proliferation of connected vehicles has transformed road-side infrastructure into an active participant in content distribution. In Content-Centric Vehicular Networks (CCVNs), Road-Side Units (RSUs) serve as edge caching nodes that deliver requested content to vehicles via Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) links. Because vehicles traverse an RSU's communication coverage in a matter of seconds to tens of seconds, content that is not available locally at the RSU at the moment of the request cannot be fully served before the vehicle exits coverage. Proactive caching — placing content at the RSU before the demand materializes — is therefore essential to service continuity. The effectiveness of any proactive caching strategy is gated on one quantity: how long a particular vehicle will remain within the RSU's coverage zone. Without an accurate estimate of this sojourn duration, a caching scheduler cannot determine whether sufficient time exists to complete a transfer, nor can it prioritize among competing content requests. Accurate, low-latency prediction of RSU dwell time at the moment a content request is issued is thus a foundational requirement for CCVN precaching systems.

Existing work addresses related problems but leaves three structural gaps when evaluated against this requirement. First, vehicular mobility prediction methods — including LSTM-based trajectory predictors [C2-1], spatial-temporal graph convolutional networks [C2-6, C2-7], and multi-vehicle interaction models [C2-2] — rely on continuous time-series inputs assembled from periodic, globally collected sensor data. These architectures are not applicable at a single event-driven snapshot: when a content request arrives, there is no time-series history available at the RSU, only the current state of the vehicle and its local surroundings. Adapting sequence-based methods to this setting requires either a buffering delay that is incompatible with precaching latency constraints, or a degraded single-frame inference for which these models were not designed. Second, proactive caching research in vehicular and mobile edge networks [C1-1, C1-3, C1-4, C1-5, C3-3, C3-4] addresses content placement without predicting dwell time as an explicit intermediate quantity. Popularity-based optimization [C1-1, C1-4] and deep reinforcement learning policies [C1-5, C3-4] determine what to cache, but they cannot answer when a transfer must begin and when it must complete — decisions that require a scalar dwell-time estimate with quantified confidence. Methods that couple mobility with caching [C3-1, C3-3, C3-7] map coarse-grained location predictions to placement decisions but do not produce the fine-grained sojourn duration needed to schedule individual transfers. Third, virtually all surveyed approaches assume a centralized server that aggregates observations across vehicles and road segments [C2-5, C2-6, C3-2, C3-7]. In a deployed RSU network, such aggregation introduces communication overhead and inference latency that scale with network size. RSU-local inference, without dependence on a central coordinator, is necessary to keep response time within the window of a single content request event.

To address these gaps, we propose ST-MBAN (Spatio-Temporal Multi-Branch Attention Network), a deterministic dwell-time prediction model designed for event-driven, RSU-local inference in CCVN environments. ST-MBAN is triggered by content request events: when a vehicle issues a request to its current RSU, a snapshot of the vehicle's state is captured and processed immediately, eliminating any buffering requirement. Each RSU trains and runs its own model instance independently, using observations collected solely within its coverage zone. This design resolves the first and third gaps: event-driven snapshot inference removes the dependency on time-series continuity, and RSU-local learning removes the dependency on a central server, allowing each model to implicitly internalize the signal cycle timing and road geometry of its intersection. To resolve the second gap, ST-MBAN predicts two scalar dwell times — the remaining sojourn at the current RSU and the full sojourn at the next RSU — as direct regression targets, providing the caching scheduler with the quantities it requires to schedule and prioritize transfers. The model encodes its input through three semantically separated branches — Kinematic (K), Traffic Control (T), and Social/Contextual (S) — and fuses the resulting branch representations through a multi-head feature attention mechanism, enabling the model to concentrate on the dominant factors governing sojourn duration under varying traffic conditions.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

- **Deterministic prediction for dwell-time estimation.** We replace the conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE) formulation used in the predecessor ST-CVAE model with a fully deterministic regression architecture. Dwell time is a physically determined quantity given the state of the vehicle and its environment; the stochastic latent space of a CVAE introduces unnecessary variance that degrades point-estimate accuracy. Empirical comparisons confirm that the CVAE component in ST-CVAE converges to behavior approximating a multi-layer perceptron, motivating this transition.

- **Three-branch input decomposition.** We partition the input feature space into three semantically distinct branches — Kinematic (K: vehicle speed, distance-to-boundary, leader-follower gaps), Traffic Control (T: signal state, phase countdown), and Social/Contextual (S: queue lengths, vehicle density, lane occupancy) — and encode each branch with a dedicated encoder block. This decomposition allows each encoder to apply processing suited to the statistical structure of its inputs, rather than mixing heterogeneous features in a single flat representation.

- **Multi-head feature attention fusion.** We introduce a three-token multi-head attention mechanism that operates on the branch-level representations $[Z_k, Z_t, Z_s]$ to produce a fused embedding. This mechanism allows the model to adaptively weight branch contributions according to the current traffic context — shifting attention toward kinematic features under free-flow conditions and toward social features under congested conditions — without requiring explicit regime labels.

- **SUMO-derived microscopic input variables.** We augment the input feature set with nine variables obtained from the SUMO microscopic traffic simulator that are not present in prior RSU dwell-time prediction formulations. These variables — including mean speed of leading vehicles, distance to the lead vehicle, estimated edge traversal time, halting vehicle queue lengths at current and next intersections, lane occupancy rates, and the number of vehicles ahead of the requesting vehicle — capture fine-grained local traffic dynamics that have direct causal influence on sojourn duration.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews related work on vehicular caching, mobility prediction, and combined mobility-caching approaches. Section III formalizes the system model, defines the prediction task, and enumerates the input feature space. Section IV describes the ST-MBAN architecture in detail. Section V presents the simulation setup, dataset collection procedure, and experimental results. Section VI concludes the paper.
